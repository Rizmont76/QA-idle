**Owner Role:** Senior Game Designer
**Status:** Frozen v1.0

This document describes the emotional and psychological journey of the player throughout QA Idle.

It focuses on what the player sees, feels, expects, discovers and wants next.

This document does not define exact mechanics, numbers, formulas, prices or UI layouts.

---

# Designer Notes

## DN-01: Add emotional names to each career stage

### Proposal

Each career stage should have a clear emotional theme, not only a mechanical unlock.

Example:

* Junior QA — Curiosity and first control
* Middle QA — Relief and first support
* Senior QA — Competence and professional pride
* QA Lead — Responsibility and ownership
* Head of QA / Director — Strategic pressure and authority
* CTO / Own Company — Power and scale
* Prestige — Legacy and rebirth

### Pros

* Helps UX, UI and economy documents align around the same emotional goal.
* Makes each promotion feel like a chapter, not a stat gate.
* Supports the vision that career progression is the real reward.

### Cons

* May constrain future mechanics if stage emotions become too rigid.
* Requires UI, copywriting and progression to consistently support these emotions.

---

## DN-02: Treat interface expansion as an emotional reward

### Proposal

Whenever a new system unlocks, the UI should not simply add a tab.
It should feel like the player's workplace has expanded.

Examples:

* Junior QA sees a simple personal workspace.
* Middle QA sees first team-related area.
* Senior QA sees more professional tooling.
* QA Lead sees management dashboards.
* Director sees department / contract views.
* Company owner sees business-scale panels.

### Pros

* Strongly supports the principle that UI is gameplay.
* Makes growth visible even before the player understands all numbers.
* Creates “wow” moments without needing expensive art.

### Cons

* Requires careful UI pacing.
* Too much visual expansion can overwhelm the player if introduced too quickly.

---

## DN-03: Add “anticipation windows” before promotions

### Proposal

Before a promotion, the game may lightly tease the emotional promise of the next stage without revealing full mechanics.

Example:

> “Your reports are getting noticed. Soon, you may not be testing alone.”

### Pros

* Builds motivation.
* Makes promotions feel earned narratively.
* Helps the player understand why they are grinding toward the next rank.

### Cons

* If too explicit, it may break the rule that future mechanics should not be shown too early.
* Needs careful wording.

---

## DN-04: Preserve manual testing emotionally, even when it becomes less optimal

### Proposal

Manual testing should remain symbolically important throughout the journey.

Even when team, automation and company systems dominate production, the player should occasionally be reminded:

> “This all started with one manually found bug.”

### Pros

* Keeps emotional continuity.
* Prevents early gameplay from feeling obsolete.
* Strengthens the career fantasy.

### Cons

* If overused mechanically, manual clicking may compete with better idle systems.
* Needs to stay emotional or optional rather than mandatory.

# 1. Purpose of the Player Journey

The purpose of the Player Journey document is to define how QA Idle should feel across the full career path.

QA Idle is not only about increasing Bugs, Money or Reputation.
The player should feel that their identity inside the game changes over time.

The core emotional promise is:

> “I started as a Junior QA, and now I am responsible for quality at a scale I could not imagine before.”

The player journey must support the project goal that progression feels like career growth rather than only larger numbers.

The player should repeatedly feel:

* “I understand what I am doing.”
* “I became better at this job.”
* “Something new just opened.”
* “My role changed.”
* “The game itself grew because I got promoted.”
* “I want to see what the next rank unlocks.”

This aligns with the core vision that promotions, interface expansion and new gameplay layers should matter more than simple numerical upgrades.

---

# 2. Core Emotional Arc

The full emotional journey of QA Idle follows this structure:

```text
Confusion / Curiosity
    ↓
First Competence
    ↓
Efficiency
    ↓
Trust
    ↓
Responsibility
    ↓
Authority
    ↓
Strategic Control
    ↓
Ownership
    ↓
Absurd Scale
    ↓
Legacy / Prestige
```

The player should not feel this shift all at once.

The game begins grounded and simple.
Then, with each career stage, the player’s emotional relationship to the game changes.

At first, the player thinks:

> “I am finding bugs.”

Later, they think:

> “I am building a QA system.”

Eventually, they think:

> “I am running quality at an impossible scale.”

# 3. Player Motivation Model

QA Idle should motivate the player through five major emotional drivers.

## 3.1 Discovery

Discovery is the strongest motivator.

The player wants to know:

* what the next promotion unlocks;
* what new panel appears;
* what new resource becomes important;
* how the game changes next;
* how far the QA fantasy can escalate.

The game should create curiosity without showing everything too early.

The player should often think:

> “Wait, there is more?”

## 3.2 Career Identity

The player should feel that their title matters.

A promotion should not feel like a label.
It should feel like the player now has a different job.

Junior QA, Senior QA, QA Lead, Director and Company Owner should each create different emotions.

The title should communicate:

* current responsibility;
* current scale;
* current fantasy;
* next ambition.

## 3.3 Visible Growth

The player should see growth through the interface.

A small UI at the beginning supports the feeling of being a beginner.

A larger UI later supports the feeling of managing more responsibility.

The interface should visually prove that the player’s career expanded.

## 3.4 Efficiency Satisfaction

The player should enjoy making the system work better.

This includes:

* finding better upgrade paths;
* improving passive production;
* making automation stronger;
* growing team output;
* completing contracts faster;
* preparing for prestige.

Optimization should feel satisfying, not stressful.

## 3.5 Long-Term Curiosity

The player should always have a reason to return.

This reason may be:

* a nearly affordable upgrade;
* a nearly completed promotion;
* a newly unlocked system;
* offline progress waiting;
* a contract close to completion;
* a prestige reward preview.

The player should usually leave the game thinking:

> “Next time I open it, I can probably unlock something.”

# 4. Journey Principles

## 4.1 The player must never feel lost

Even when the game becomes larger, the current goal must remain understandable.

At any stage, the player should know:

* what they can do now;
* why it matters;
* what they are working toward;
* what changed after the last unlock.

Complexity may increase, but confusion should not.

## 4.2 The player must not see the whole game too early

Future systems should remain hidden until relevant.

This protects:

* simplicity;
* surprise;
* motivation;
* clean UI;
* career fantasy.

The player should discover the game in layers.

## 4.3 Each promotion must create an emotional shift

A promotion should answer:

> “How does my life as a QA change now?”

Not only:

> “What multiplier did I receive?”

A good promotion changes the player's feeling of role, power or responsibility.

## 4.4 New mechanics should feel like new responsibilities

When a system unlocks, it should not feel random.

Team, Automation, Reputation, Contracts, Company and Prestige should feel like natural consequences of career growth.

The player should think:

> “Of course this is what I deal with now. I got promoted.”

## 4.5 Idle progress should feel like the world continues without pressure

Offline progress should feel rewarding and relaxing.

The player should feel:

> “My systems worked while I was away.”

Not:

> “I failed because I was not online.”

# 5. Stage 1 — Junior QA

## 5.1 Emotional Theme

**Curiosity, simplicity and first control.**

The player is new.
The game is small.
The world is understandable.

The player should feel like they are personally doing QA work.

## 5.2 What the player sees

The player sees a minimal interface:

* Bugs Found;
* Money;
* manual testing action;
* report action;
* simple shop;
* basic stats;
* next promotion goal.

There should be no visible team, automation, contracts, company or prestige systems yet.

The screen should feel like a personal workspace rather than a management dashboard.

## 5.3 What the player feels

The player should feel:

* “I understand this.”
* “I click, I find bugs.”
* “I report bugs and earn money.”
* “I can improve.”
* “I am at the beginning of a career.”

This stage should feel approachable and safe.

## 5.4 What motivates the player

The main motivation is immediate cause and effect.

The player sees that:

```text
Testing creates bugs.
Bugs become money.
Money buys upgrades.
Upgrades make testing better.
```

The first upgrades should create quick satisfaction.

The player should feel:

> “I am already better than when I started.”

## 5.5 Curiosity hook

The main curiosity hook is the first promotion.

The player should wonder:

> “What happens when I become Middle QA?”

The next rank should be visible, but the full future should not be revealed.

## 5.6 Desired emotional outcome

By the end of Junior QA, the player should feel:

* confident with the basic loop;
* attached to Bugs Found as the iconic resource;
* ready for something more;
* excited that promotion may change the game.

The player should not feel that the game has already shown everything.

---

# 6. Stage 2 — Middle QA

## 6.1 Emotional Theme

**Relief, support and first collaboration.**

The player is no longer alone.

This is the first major emotional expansion: progress can now come from systems beyond manual clicking.

## 6.2 What the player sees

The interface expands for the first time.

The player may now see:

* team-related area;
* passive bug generation;
* first helper or assistant concept;
* more upgrade options;
* clearer production stats.

The UI should visibly communicate:

> “Your role has grown.”

## 6.3 What the player feels

The player should feel:

* “I got promoted.”
* “The game opened up.”
* “I am not doing everything alone anymore.”
* “My work is becoming more scalable.”

This is the first real “wow” moment.

## 6.4 What motivates the player

The player is motivated by the shift from effort to support.

Manual testing still matters, but the player starts to enjoy watching progress happen passively.

The emotional reward is relief:

> “I built something that works even when I am not constantly clicking.”

## 6.5 Curiosity hook

The player should begin wondering:

* “How big can my team become?”
* “What other systems will appear?”
* “What does Senior QA unlock?”
* “Will I get better tools?”

## 6.6 Desired emotional outcome

By the end of Middle QA, the player should feel:

* that the first promotion truly mattered;
* that idle progression has begun;
* that the game can evolve;
* that the next rank may unlock an even deeper system.

---

# 7. Stage 3 — Senior QA

## 7.1 Emotional Theme

**Competence, trust and professional pride.**

The player now feels experienced.

The fantasy shifts from:

> “I do QA tasks.”

to:

> “I understand quality and can improve the system.”

## 7.2 What the player sees

The interface becomes more professional.

The player may see:

* Reputation;
* Automation;
* advanced upgrades;
* stronger stats;
* more meaningful production sources;
* improved offline progress indicators.

The UI should feel less like a simple clicker and more like a QA toolkit.

## 7.3 What the player feels

The player should feel:

* “I am trusted now.”
* “I have real professional tools.”
* “I am not just working harder; I am working smarter.”
* “Automation changes the game.”

Automation should feel like a major unlock, not just a passive income source.

## 7.4 What motivates the player

The player is motivated by professional mastery.

They now make decisions between:

* personal efficiency;
* team output;
* automation;
* reputation growth;
* promotion preparation.

The emotional reward is competence.

The player should feel smart for investing well.

## 7.5 Curiosity hook

The player should wonder:

* “How strong can automation become?”
* “What does Reputation unlock?”
* “What happens when I become responsible for people?”
* “Will the game become more managerial?”

## 7.6 Desired emotional outcome

By the end of Senior QA, the player should feel:

* proud of their systems;
* comfortable with multiple resources;
* ready for leadership;
* curious about larger responsibility.

---

# 8. Stage 4 — QA Lead

## 8.1 Emotional Theme

**Responsibility, ownership and people management.**

This is a major psychological shift.

The player is no longer only improving their own output.
They are responsible for how the team works.

## 8.2 What the player sees

The UI should become more managerial.

The player may see:

* deeper team management;
* process-oriented upgrades;
* team efficiency indicators;
* QA strategy elements;
* more structured goals.

The screen should communicate:

> “You now manage quality through people and process.”

## 8.3 What the player feels

The player should feel:

* “My decisions affect more than myself.”
* “I am shaping the team.”
* “The game is becoming strategic.”
* “I have more responsibility now.”

This stage should feel slightly more serious, but not stressful.

## 8.4 What motivates the player

The motivation comes from ownership.

The player wants to make the team better.

They are no longer asking:

> “How do I find more bugs?”

They are asking:

> “How do I make the whole QA process stronger?”

## 8.5 Curiosity hook

The player should wonder:

* “What happens when my responsibility expands beyond one team?”
* “Will I manage departments?”
* “Will I work with bigger clients?”
* “What does Head of QA unlock?”

## 8.6 Desired emotional outcome

By the end of QA Lead, the player should feel:

* responsible;
* respected;
* invested in team scaling;
* ready for department-level gameplay.

---

# 9. Stage 5 — Head of QA / Director

## 9.1 Emotional Theme

**Authority, strategy and organizational scale.**

The player is now above day-to-day QA execution.

The fantasy shifts from team management to department leadership.

## 9.2 What the player sees

The interface should feel broader.

The player may see:

* contracts;
* department-level goals;
* larger reward structures;
* reputation-based opportunities;
* organizational upgrades;
* quality KPIs or strategic indicators.

The UI should communicate:

> “You are now making decisions at company scale.”

## 9.3 What the player feels

The player should feel:

* “I am important now.”
* “Clients and departments depend on my QA systems.”
* “My decisions have larger consequences.”
* “The game has moved beyond clicking.”

The player should feel powerful, but still motivated by the next layer.

## 9.4 What motivates the player

The main motivation becomes medium-term goals.

Contracts are emotionally important because they give the player something larger to complete.

The player should feel:

> “I am not only buying upgrades. I am delivering major QA value.”

## 9.5 Curiosity hook

The player should wonder:

* “What happens after I reach the executive level?”
* “Can I create my own QA company?”
* “How large can this become?”
* “Where does the absurd scale begin?”

## 9.6 Desired emotional outcome

By the end of this stage, the player should feel:

* authoritative;
* strategic;
* prepared for ownership;
* excited for a larger fantasy.

---

# 10. Stage 6 — CTO / Own QA Company

## 10.1 Emotional Theme

**Power, ownership and empire building.**

The player is no longer simply progressing through a career.

They are building something of their own.

## 10.2 What the player sees

The interface should now feel like a business or organization view.

The player may see:

* company systems;
* company reputation;
* large contracts;
* expansion opportunities;
* prestige preparation;
* broader scale indicators.

The screen should communicate:

> “You are now running the machine.”

## 10.3 What the player feels

The player should feel:

* “I made it.”
* “This started small, but now I control the whole QA operation.”
* “My career became a company.”
* “I am close to something bigger than this run.”

This should be one of the strongest emotional payoff points in the game.

## 10.4 What motivates the player

The player is motivated by scale and legacy.

They want to:

* complete major goals;
* grow company reputation;
* reach prestige requirements;
* see how far the fantasy escalates;
* prepare for a stronger next cycle.

## 10.5 Curiosity hook

The player should wonder:

* “What happens after prestige?”
* “What permanent power do I keep?”
* “How much faster will the next run be?”
* “What new possibilities appear after reset?”

## 10.6 Desired emotional outcome

By the end of this stage, the player should feel:

* powerful;
* accomplished;
* curious about prestige;
* emotionally ready for a reset because it feels like evolution, not loss.

---

# 11. Prestige Journey

## 11.1 Emotional Theme

**Legacy, rebirth and long-term ambition.**

Prestige should not feel like punishment.

It should feel like the player is converting one successful career into a stronger foundation for the next one.

## 11.2 What the player sees

Before prestige, the player should clearly see:

* what will reset;
* what will remain;
* what permanent bonus will be gained;
* why the next run will be better;
* whether prestige is optional.

There should be no surprise loss.

## 11.3 What the player feels

The player should feel:

* “I achieved something meaningful.”
* “I am not losing everything.”
* “The next run will be faster or deeper.”
* “This is the beginning of a larger journey.”

Prestige should create anticipation, not regret.

## 11.4 What motivates the player

The motivation is long-term mastery.

The player wants to repeat the journey with:

* stronger bonuses;
* faster early progress;
* new unlocks;
* new branches;
* larger scale;
* deeper optimization.

## 11.5 Desired emotional outcome

After prestiging, the player should feel:

* nostalgic for the first run;
* satisfied by faster progress;
* curious about new possibilities;
* motivated to rebuild better.

The emotional message should be:

> “You are not starting over. You are starting above where you once began.”

# 12. Interface Growth as Player Journey

The interface is part of the player's emotional progression.

At the beginning, the UI should be small because the player’s role is small.

As the player advances, the UI expands because their responsibilities expand.

This creates a direct emotional connection:

```text
More career responsibility
    ↓
More visible interface
    ↓
More systems to manage
    ↓
Stronger feeling of growth
```

## 12.1 Early UI emotion

The early UI should feel:

* simple;
* personal;
* readable;
* direct;
* low-pressure.

The player should not feel overwhelmed.

## 12.2 Mid-game UI emotion

The mid-game UI should feel:

* more capable;
* more professional;
* more layered;
* more efficient.

The player should feel that they now have tools and people.

## 12.3 Late-game UI emotion

The late-game UI should feel:

* strategic;
* powerful;
* organizational;
* large-scale.

The player should feel that they are no longer operating as one person.

## 12.4 Prestige UI emotion

Prestige UI should feel:

* clear;
* ceremonial;
* safe;
* rewarding.

The player should never wonder whether prestige is a trap.

# 13. Discovery Moments

Discovery moments are key emotional peaks.

A discovery moment happens when the player unlocks something that changes their understanding of the game.

Examples:

* first manual upgrade;
* first promotion;
* first team member;
* first passive production;
* first automation system;
* first reputation gate;
* first contract;
* first company system;
* first prestige preview;
* first absurd-scale expansion.

Each discovery moment should have:

* clear feedback;
* short explanation;
* visible UI change;
* emotional framing;
* immediate reason to interact with the new system.

The player should not only understand what unlocked.
They should feel why it matters.

---

# 14. Curiosity Design

Curiosity should be created through controlled mystery.

The player should know the next goal, but not the entire future.

Good curiosity:

> “Promotion to Middle QA is close. Something new will unlock.”

Bad curiosity:

> “Here are 14 locked tabs you cannot use yet.”

The game should avoid overwhelming the player with future systems.

Instead, it should create a rhythm:

```text
Understand current loop
    ↓
Approach promotion
    ↓
Anticipate change
    ↓
Unlock new layer
    ↓
Learn new layer
    ↓
Feel stronger
    ↓
Repeat
```

# 15. Emotional Pacing

QA Idle should alternate between comfort and surprise.

## 15.1 Comfort

Comfort comes from:

* familiar resource growth;
* understandable upgrades;
* idle progress;
* clear goals;
* predictable improvement.

Comfort makes the game relaxing.

## 15.2 Surprise

Surprise comes from:

* promotions;
* new systems;
* new resources;
* UI expansion;
* humorous QA-themed unlocks;
* absurd scale escalation.

Surprise makes the game exciting.

## 15.3 Ideal rhythm

The ideal emotional rhythm is:

```text
Comfortable loop
    ↓
Small upgrade satisfaction
    ↓
Clear next goal
    ↓
Promotion anticipation
    ↓
Major unlock surprise
    ↓
Short learning moment
    ↓
New comfortable loop
```

This rhythm should repeat across the whole game.

---

# 16. Humor in the Player Journey

Humor should support the fantasy, not replace it.

The player should occasionally smile because the game understands software development culture.

Humor can appear in:

* upgrade names;
* promotion messages;
* achievement names;
* contract descriptions;
* automation flavor text;
* absurd late-game scale.

The tone should be light and relatable.

The game should avoid becoming a pure parody too early.

The early career should feel believable.
The absurdity should grow gradually.

---

# 17. Learning Without Teaching

QA Idle may introduce real QA concepts, but the player should not feel like they are reading a textbook.

The player can learn through:

* upgrade names;
* short descriptions;
* system names;
* progression context;
* humorous explanations;
* natural consequences.

Example emotional effect:

> “I do not fully know what test design techniques are, but it sounds useful, and now I understand that it helps me find better bugs.”

The learning experience should create curiosity, not pressure.

---

# 18. Return Player Experience

When the player returns after being offline, the desired feeling is:

> “My QA systems kept working.”

The return experience should show:

* what was gained;
* why it was gained;
* what can now be bought;
* what goal is closer;
* whether a new unlock is near.

The return should quickly lead to a meaningful decision.

The player should not only collect resources.
They should immediately think:

> “Now I can do something.”

---

# 19. Session Ending Experience

A good session should end with unfinished motivation.

The player should leave with one of these thoughts:

* “I almost have enough for the next upgrade.”
* “I am close to promotion.”
* “My automation is finally starting to work.”
* “Next time I can complete this contract.”
* “I want to see what prestige gives me.”
* “The next rank sounds interesting.”

The game should avoid sessions ending in confusion.

The player should always know why returning will be satisfying.

---

# 20. Psychological Risks to Avoid

## 20.1 Too much complexity too early

If the player sees too many systems at the start, the Junior QA fantasy breaks.

The player may feel like they opened a spreadsheet, not started a career.

## 20.2 Promotions that only increase numbers

If a promotion only gives a multiplier, the core fantasy weakens.

Promotions must feel like role changes.

## 20.3 Automation replacing the player too quickly

Automation should feel empowering, not like the player became irrelevant.

Manual QA should remain emotionally respected as the foundation.

## 20.4 Prestige feeling like loss

If prestige is unclear, players may avoid it or feel punished.

Prestige must be previewed, optional and rewarding.

## 20.5 Absurd scale appearing too suddenly

Planet-wide or interplanetary QA can be funny and exciting, but only if the player emotionally accepts the escalation.

The transition must be gradual.

# 22. Player Journey Requirements for Future Documents

Future UX, UI, economy and progression documents should follow these rules:

1. Every career stage must have a clear emotional goal.
2. Every major unlock must create a visible change in player experience.
3. The interface should expand only when the player’s role expands.
4. The player should always understand their current goal.
5. Future systems should stay hidden until relevant.
6. Promotions should feel like new chapters.
7. Automation should feel empowering, not replacing.
8. Team systems should feel collaborative, not factory-like.
9. Contracts should feel like meaningful professional opportunities.
10. Prestige should feel like legacy, not punishment.
11. Humor should support the QA fantasy.
12. The absurd scale should escalate gradually.
13. Numbers should support emotional progression, not replace it.

---

# 23. Final Player Journey Statement

QA Idle should make the player feel that they are living through an expanding QA career.

The journey begins with one person manually finding bugs.

It grows into teamwork, automation, leadership, contracts, company ownership and eventually absurd levels of QA responsibility.

The emotional core is not:

> “My numbers became bigger.”

The emotional core is:

> “My role changed, my interface expanded, and the game became bigger because I became bigger.”
